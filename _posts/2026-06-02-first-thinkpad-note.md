I learned early in college that there are forms of knowledge that exist outside the classroom. They live in passing comments from families who know how higher education works, in advice shared around dinner tables, and in the confidence that comes from already understanding the rules of a system before entering it. My parents did not go to college and began their lives in the real world almost straight out of high school, which is pretty common for that time and place in the community I am from. 

When I first arrived in college, I often felt like I was navigating something important without fully understanding the informal map many others already seemed to know. I was always told that excelling at school should be my top priority if I want to claim my place, but I soon realized that for most people around me this notion was not their motto. 

I remember sitting in academic advising meetings during my first year, writing down terms I did not understand so I could look them up later in private. Office hours, networking, research opportunities, internships, these concepts were introduced as if they were obvious, as if everyone had inherited a working vocabulary for academia. I performed poorly in my first internship, despite doing quite well in my classes. What looked from the outside like confidence in my peers often felt, to me, like fluency in a language I had only just begun learning. 

At the time, I interpreted confusion as evidence that I was behind. There were moments when I interpreted my struggle to understand the hidden curriculum of college as evidence that I did not belong in it. I was juggling three student jobs at the time to make ends meet, which left less room for early professional exploration compared to some of my classmates. My instinct became observation. I watched how others asked questions, how they spoke to professors, how they navigated uncertainty. I learned through imitation first and confidence second. 

What I did not realize then was that this process of entering unfamiliar systems, identifying invisible rules, and slowly building fluency would become one of the defining patterns of my life.

Years later, now PhD student studying statistical and behavioral genetics, I spend much of my time thinking about complexity. A large part of my research work center around the idea that our constructs for understanding complex disease, especially psychiatric disease, rarely follow simple patterns. Genetic signals emerge from noise, associations are probabilistic rather than deterministic, and meaningful findings often require sitting with ambiguity for long periods before clarity emerges. This work has taught me that uncertainty is not a sign that something is wrong; it is often evidence that you are working at the edge of what is known. The same frameworks I use to study human behavior have given me language for experiences I once understood only as failure.

For me, this lesson does not stay confined to the lab.

There is a particular kind of discomfort that comes with not knowing whether it is not understanding a statistical method, not knowing how academia works, or not knowing if you belong in the room you worked hard to enter. Earlier in my education, uncertainty felt personal. Now, I understand it differently. Confusion has become information rather than indictment.

Some of the resilience I developed as a first-generation student came from necessity. I learned how to find information when no one around me had the answers. I learned how to tolerate being new (and bad) at things for longer than I wanted to be. I learned that many systems appear intuitive only after you understand their architecture.

My scientific pursuits strengthened these skills in a different way. Scientific progress is iterative and often slow. Analyses fail. Hypotheses change. Results complicate the stories we hoped to tell. Working in science has repeatedly forced me to separate my identity from immediate success; to see setbacks as part of inquiry rather than evidence of inadequacy. Over time, this changed how I approached challenges beyond research as well.

When I think about resilience now, I think less about perseverance in the dramatic sense and more about repetition. It is the decision to return to difficult problems after unproductive days. It is learning new things despite feeling behind. It is asking questions earlier instead of waiting until I feel qualified enough to ask them. It is recognizing that competence often looks, from the inside, like prolonged uncertainty.

Being first-generation shaped how I entered academia, but it also shaped how I move through it now. I notice when expectations go unstated because I once depended on noticing. I value and think carefully about mentorship, because I feel empowered by all the people who chose to invest in my growth. And I am increasingly interested not only in studying complex systems, but in helping make complex systems more navigable for those who come after me.

I once thought resilience meant eventually feeling certain. Instead, I have found that resilience is learning how to continue moving through uncertainty without letting it define your sense of capability. Both my path into higher education and my work in statistical genetics have taught me the same lesson: understanding rarely arrives all at once. More often, it is built gradually—in questions asked quietly, skills learned imperfectly, and the willingness to remain present long enough for complexity to become familiarity.
